# I Like JPGs — Interview Guide

## Purpose

This guide is for Milestone 0 validation conversations.

The goal is to learn whether collectors, artists, curators, and community people are interested in a taste-based read of an NFT wallet.

We are not trying to sell the product.  
We are trying to understand whether the idea feels clear, desirable, accurate, and useful.

## Short Intro

I’m testing a concept called I Like JPGs.

It gives people a taste read of an NFT wallet — not portfolio value, rarity, floor prices, or trading advice.

The idea is that what someone collects can reveal cultural signals: artists, collections, memes, communities, visual taste, identity objects, and patterns of participation.

I’m trying to understand whether that kind of read would be interesting, accurate, or useful.

## Warm-Up Questions

1. When you look at someone’s wallet, what do you usually notice first?

2. Do you think a wallet can say something meaningful about someone’s taste?

3. What kinds of NFT signals feel most meaningful to you?
   - Artists
   - Collections
   - PFPs
   - Memes
   - Editions
   - 1/1s
   - Generative art
   - Access passes
   - Communities
   - On-chain work
   - Event tokens
   - Other

4. Are there things in a wallet that you think people often misread?

## Concept Questions

5. When you hear “a taste read for your NFT wallet,” what do you think that means?

6. Does that sound interesting, confusing, useful, or unnecessary?

7. What would you expect to see in a wallet taste read?

8. Would you be curious to see a read of your own wallet?

9. Would you enter or share a wallet address to get one?

## Accuracy Questions

10. What would make a wallet read feel accurate?

11. What would make it feel wrong or too reductive?

12. Would you rather the product say:
    - “This wallet is X”
    - “This wallet seems drawn toward X”
    - “This wallet shows signals of X”

13. How much explanation or proof would you need to trust the read?

14. Would seeing proof NFTs and top collections make the read feel more believable?

## Category Questions

15. Do these categories make sense?

- Art
- Generative
- PFP / Identity
- Meme / Internet Culture
- Gaming / Worlds
- Access / Membership
- Collectibles
- Music / Media
- Unsorted Signals

16. Are any categories missing?

17. Are any categories confusing or too broad?

18. Should one NFT be able to belong to multiple categories?

## Social Questions

19. Would you share a wallet read publicly?

20. Would you compare your wallet with another collector’s wallet?

21. Would this help you discover people you might want to meet?

22. Would this help you discover artists, collections, clubs, or communities?

23. Could you imagine using something like this at an event, dinner, club, or collector gathering?

## Trust / Boundaries

24. What should this product avoid?

25. What would make it feel creepy, overly financial, or too judgmental?

26. Should the read include uncertainty when the metadata is thin or unclear?

27. Would you trust a product more if it showed why it made each claim?

## Closing Questions

28. After hearing the idea, would you want to see your own wallet read?

29. Is there a wallet you would want interpreted?

30. Who else should I talk to about this?

31. What would make this product genuinely useful instead of just interesting?

## Strong Validation Signals

A strong positive signal is when someone says:

- “I want to see mine.”
- “Can you run my wallet?”
- “I want to compare with someone.”
- “This would be useful at an event.”
- “This could help me find people with similar taste.”
- “This is more interesting than portfolio tools.”

## Weak Signals

A weak signal is when someone:

- Only says “cool” but does not want to try it
- Thinks it is just another NFT portfolio tool
- Focuses entirely on price or rarity
- Does not understand why taste matters
- Would not share or enter a wallet
- Does not trust the interpretation

## Notes For Interviewer

Listen for the exact words people use.

Do not over-explain.  
Do not defend the idea.  
Do not correct them too quickly.

The most useful feedback is often where they misunderstand the concept.
